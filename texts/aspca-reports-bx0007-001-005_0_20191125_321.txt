REPORT OF THE EXECUTIVE COMMITTEE.

No extended report of this branch of the Institution is
necessary, as its duties mainly consist in its monthly transac-
tions in Committee, always recorded in the minutes of the
Society.

It is, however, the Committee’s melancholy duty to ofﬁcially
announce the decease of two of the earliest friends and incorp-
orators of the Society during the past year, namely: the Rev.
Henry W. Bellows and Mr. Moses Taylor.

The Committee also report the election of Messrs. Thomas
C. Action and Alfred Wagstaff as Vice-Presidents of the Society,
to ﬁll the vacancies caused by the death of the late Rev. Henry
W. Bellows and Mr. Moses Taylor; also the electionof Mr.
J. W. Edwards as Assistant Treasurer; also the resignation of
Mr. Frederic Gallatin as Secretary, and the election of Mr.
Henry Bergh, Jr., to ﬁll that vacancy.

Upon the scroll preserved in the Institution are seen the
names of'seventy among the most eminent citizens of New
York who came to the aid of Mr. Bergh at the beginning of
his humane undertaking, forty-two of whom have passed away
from the sphere of their exalted usefulness.

Perhaps if those departed friends of the humble creatures
.they wished to beneﬁt could be interrogated, they would reit-
erate their approval ,of the purposes and practices of the merci-
ful Institution whose Seventeenth Anniversary expires to-day.

, SE CRETARY’S REPORT.

No better illustration of the beneﬁts to be derived from
organizations for the protection of animals can be offered, than
by comparing the present condition and treatment of animals
with that which existed seventeen years ago.

BROOKLYN AGENCY.

The establishment of an Agency in Brooklyn has largely
extended the Society’s sphere of usefulness, and the support it
has received from the public of that city is most gratifying.
The following report will give an idea of the work that has
been accomplished there during the past year, under the direc-
tion of its Superintendent:

Number of arrests... . . ....... . ........................ 277
Number of turn outs ............ . ..................... 474
Animals destroyed ................................. 352
Complaints investigated ............................. 529

SPECIAL AGENTS.

More than thirty agents have been appointed during the
last twelve months, in addition to those already existing in the
different counties of the State.

AMBULANCES.

Two handsome ambulances for the removal of disabled ani-
mals from the streets have been recently constructed, one of
which is located in Brooklyn to supply a long existing neces-
sity in that city. These vehicles have been the means of saving
the lives of hundreds of animals, and have conveyed to places
of shelter no less than 214 during the past year.

 




 

SUPERINTENDENT’S REPORT.

In submitting the following Report of the general work
accomplished by the Society during the past year, the Superin—
tendent takes occasion to say that it is impossible to enumerate
therein all the instances where its representatives have been
required to interpose on behalf of animals cruelly treated.

WORK ALONG THE CANALS.

The condition of the animals employed on the several
canals of the State has improved under the constant supervision
of our ofﬁcers residing in the towns adjacent thereto. The
sight'of horses and mules compelled to draw heavily loaded
boats with parts of the harness sometimes embedded in the
ﬂesh is becoming a thing of the past.

DOG AND COCK FIGHTING.

For some time past, the mOre isolated parts of Long Island
have ’been the resorts of dog and cock ﬁghters, and buildings
especially adapted to that brutal pastime have been erected
and maintained in some instances through the neglect, if not
connivance, of the authorities sworn to suppress them. Being
in constant fear of an attack by the Society’s ofﬁcers, the pro-
prietors of these vile places are ever on the watch, rendering it
difﬁcult to capture them in the act.

A new plan of action was at last adopted and put into suc-
cessful operation on the 15th of November last, while a crowd
of rufﬁans hailing from New York City, Boston, Philadelphia
and other places, numbering two or three hundred, were gath-
ered together in a dog'pit belonging to a notorious character
near Newtown. Two of the Society’s ofﬁcers obtained admission
as spectators and procured the necessary evidence to convict
the parties, who were subsequently arrested on warrants and

ﬁned. A few escaped, but they will doubtless be captured ere
long.

A subsequent visit to the dog pit found the'place deserted
by the owner, and all the paraphernalia pertaining thereto

It is safe to say that, for some time to come at least, no dog
or cock ﬁghts will occur in that Vicinity.

DOG POUND.

The general condition of the dog pound in this city, as well
as the quality of the food supplied to the unfortunate animals
received there, has necessitated continual surveillance on the
part of the Society’s ofﬁcers.

TRANSPORTATION OF CATTLE.

For. several years efforts have been made to construct a
cattle car that would enable animals to be regularly fed and
watered in transitu, and a number of inventions intended to
accomplish that object have been tried, but, owing to many
causes, no satisfactory result has been attained. Gradually,
however, the recommendations of our President are being
accepted, and the animals are being spared the terrible suffer-
ings of transportation, by being slaughtered near their homes
and the ﬂesh brought to the East in refrigerating cars, thus
rendering it not only healthier, but more palatable as food.

NEW ORGANIZATIONS.

It is gratifying to report that many new Societies for the
Protection of Animals have come into existence in different
States of the Union during the past year, and that the ofﬁcial
emblem of the Parent Society has been adopted by the follow-
ing kindred organizations at home and abroad:

Societa Protectora de Animales y Plantas de la Habana.
Societa Zooﬁla Napoletana.

Societa Argentina Protectora de los Animales.
Sociedade Protectora dos Animaes em Lisboa.

Greenville Branch of the South Carolina Society for the Prevention
of Cruelty to Animals.
Nashville Humane Society.

By referring to the Reports of the Treasurer and Superin-
tendent, it will be seen that the Society has accomplished a
large amount of work during the year just ended, and that
those who feel an interest in the humane movement in which it

is engaged, have reason for congratulation on the substantial
aid it has received from the public.

PRESIDENT’S REPORT.

LADIES AND GENTLEMEN OF THE SOCIETY :

The Seventeenth Year of the Ofﬁcial existence Of the par-
ent Society Of America, organized for the double purpose of
protecting the defenceless inferior animals from cruelty, as well
as the moral improvement Of those who abuse them, will be
completed ere this Report reaches you. The continuous record
of its humane work, which is written in its daily transactions,
as evidenced by the absence in our streets of those painful
exhibitions of maltreatment Of animals which once character-
ized them, renders it almost superfluous for me to dwell long
upon its proceedings for the past year.

 




 

6

With a brief allusion only to a few of the principal events
of the past twelve months, I will refer you to the Report itself
for details.

And ﬁrst of all, let me congratulate you on the continued
prosperity of the blessed Institution founded by your sense of
justice and humanity, and supported by your generosity. It
continues to enjoy the conﬁdence and approval of the public at
large, as it so eminently deserves to do, and is a shining ex-
ample to all States and communities which value the practice
of mercy as an indispensable auxiliary to civilization.

LEGISLATION.

The bill presented last season to the Legislature forthe sup-
pression of the barbarous “sport” known as pigeon shooting,
again failed, by reason of the overwhelming political inﬂuence
of “the sporting fraternity.” It was introduced in the Upper
House by Senator Boyd, and referred to the Committee on
Judiciary; and in the Assembly by Hon. G. Roosevelt, and
sent to the Committee on General Laws. After sleeping in
Committee until towards the close of the session, it was reported
unfavorably, and then died, amid the disorder which the united
Press of the State asserted at the time was without parallel.

That legislators should be equal to the crime of trading in
the mutilation and death of innocent creatures in order to
beneﬁt any political party or faction is, in the judgment of all
thoughtful people, a bad omen.

Lest these reﬂections be attributed to over zeal, I beg per-
mission to reproduce the following very temperate summary of
the session referred to, from the New York Tribune of the 5th
of May last: i

“ The State Senate yesterday took up the Assembly resolution pro-
viding for ﬁnal adjournment on the 18th of this month, and, after a brief
debate, defeated it. The people will regret this action since it means
an indeﬁnite extension of a session which has been distinguished for
nothing so much as deﬁance of public opinion and neglect of public
duty. A Legislature which gave the ﬁrst six weeks of the session to

deadlocking of the most disgraceful and inexcusable sort, but has not
been able to ﬁnd time to devote to the preparation of any comprehen—
