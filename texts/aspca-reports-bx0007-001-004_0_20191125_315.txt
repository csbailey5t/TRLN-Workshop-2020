ADDRESS OF THE PRESIDENT.

LADIES AND GENTLEMEN :

The annual revolution of the planet we inhabit indicates
the eighteenth year Of the ofﬁcial existence of your Society.

It would be as difﬁcult to particularize the mighty results
your Society has achieved during the years passed away, as
it is to give form and feature to the immense labor yet remain-

ing to be done.

While it will not be disputed that the moral status of Our
country has been elevated by the widespread recognition of
the merciful treatment of animals, it is equally true that at
times and in certain localities the hideous spirit of cruelty
sometimes manifests itself in a manner most discouraging to
the efforts of civilization.

 




 

 

 

 

 

6 r

A conspicuous illustration of this unhappy truth was fur-
nished last summer in a quarter of our favored State, where,
remote fromthe besetting sins pre-eminently supposed to dwell
in dense communities, and amidst the stupendous and awe-in-
spiring wonders of one of nature’s grandest works—the mighty
cataract of Niagara——a most atrocious crime was deliberately
perpetrated.

A horse, stricken with age, and after a score of years in the
daily service of its master, was remorselessly driven to the brink
of the precipice and then plunged into the rock-bound, roaring
cataract. The quickened pulsations of the heart of every
reader at this announcement, testify the abhorrence of the per-
petrators and spectators of that revolting deed, which is at once
an insult to our generation and an indelible stain upon our
common humanity. But this is not all—for a jury acquitted the
wretch, for he cannot be justly called a man, who prepared
this diabolical spectacle I .

As an offset to this hideous exhibition of cruelty, I report
with much pleasure the steady progress of our good work in
numerous and distant parts of our country; Thirty-seven States
and Territories, including Upper and Lower Canada, have en-
rolled themselves under the banner of animal protection, and
have adopted the emblem of the parent Society and substan-
tially its laws and policy. ' ‘

While legislatures are thus crystallizing into law the hu-
_ mane sentiments of our people everywhere, small communities
are giving expression of their justice and responsibility towards
those humble beings which give to mankind more than half
the beneﬁts they enjoy. And it is particularly gratifying to
behold ladies relinquishing for the time being their reﬁned
associatiOns of a fashionable character, to give fame and money
to the merciful work. At the risk of the displeasure of one of
these generous almoners, I venture to mention one recent in-
stance in Philadelphia, where Mrs. John Easby, alone, organ-
ized a fair in that city for the beneﬁt of animals, which through
the liberality of her friends and other citizens yielded for the
Society of Pennsylvania the handsome sum of $1,163.

 

 

 

 




 

 

 

"515 :

7

When I recall to mind how, solitary and alone, the germ
of your merciful Institution lingered long amidst the neglect,
misgivings and contempt of the world, and contrast therewith
its present status, who will say we have not ample cause for
congratulation.

On the 2lst of September, 1883, the State of Alabama
joined our merciful confederacy.

CANALS.

The selﬁsh indifference. to the sufferings of horses which

drag the products of the State through the canals is as con-

spicuous as ever. As usual. ofﬁcers have been detailed to in-
vestigate that phase of cruelty, and last summer they made
many arrests, which were appropriately treated by the local
authorities. The city of Schenectady has always manifested a
marked Willingness to assist in the suppression of these inhu-
manities, and too high praise cannot be awarded to the magis-
trates of that city. This crime, however, against mercy and
the laws, will probably never cease until that antiquated system
of freight transportation shall be substituted by some motor
more in keeping with the spirit of the age. This lifeless, snail-
like clog upon Yankee enterprise should be as speedily as pos-
sible' consigned to a Pantheon of obsolete inventions, and the
sluggish waters within them emptied into the sea.

CATTLE SHIPPING.

The formidable proportions which this industry has as-
sumed of late years, calls into requisition the incessant watch-
fulness and interference of the Society. Judging by the dis-
position shown by certain shippers of live stock, and connived
at by some of the steamship companies, the conviction is irre-
sistible that without the protecting inﬂuence of this Society the
fate of these defenceless creatures would be terrible in the ex-
treme. A recent instance may be cited in support of this con-
clusion. On the 5th of December last, with a snowstorm
threatening, and which began on the third ,day following, it

 




 

 

 

 

8

was decided to ship 150 sheep on board a steamer bound to
England, all of which were to be placed upon the upper deck
without cover or protection of any kind from the sea and winter
blasts! It may be said that such a thing, so antagonistic to
personal interest, irrespective of humanity, is incredible—and so
it is, but, nevertheless it is true. This abominable disregard of
the simplest instincts of feeling and common sense being re-
ported ,to me, I forthwith addressed a letter to the commander
of the steamer, citing the law, and assuring him that in the
event of the purpose being attempted he would be arrested and
his ship probably be detained in port. This communication,
along with the presence of a strong force to vindicate the hu-
manities of the case, as well as the mandate of the State, had the
desired effect, and the vessel sailed without the perpetration of
such a merciless barbarity upon helpless animals.

CREMATION OF LIVING ANIMALS.

Those shocking tragedies, represented by the burning of
living horsesand other animals, conﬁned under ground and
upon the upper ﬂoors of stables, periodically assail the public
eye and sympathies. Almost with the regularity of the passing
months, the journals of the day record these fearful holocausts,
sometimes by the dozen, at others by hundreds. So great is
the greed of avarice, and so blind are its votaries in the pur-
suit of gain, that the suggestions of ordinary prudence fail them
completely, so far as this matter is concerned. Hence a thou-
sand horses are sometimes seen stabled upon the second. third
and even fourth ﬂoors, while the “rolling stock ” is usually stored
upon a level with the street! It may be remembered that a
bill was introduced to the Legislature two or three years ago
by us, which, had it been allowed to pass, would have almost
entirely averted these terrible disasters and loss of property,
through the interference of your Society. ' But it failed through
the adverse inﬂuence of an ambitious young senator, now
representing this country abroad. It is thought that money is
the root of all evil, but I incline to the opinion that in our
country at least it is politics.

Impressed with the belief that ﬁgures often speak more
eloquently than words, we have endeavored to submit, in as
concise a manner as possible, a statement of the actual work
accomplished by this Institution in the cause of humanity
during the year.

On this, the eighteenth anniversary of the birth of your
Society, we are enabled to report that, since its organization, it
has been instrumental in causing the'arrest of no less than
10,675 persons for violating the laws of the State which afford
protection to animals, and, in addition thereto, the cases Where
disabled animals were suspended from work and the evils reme—
died without recourse to the courts, reached a total of 24,142.

During the year just ended the aggregate of prosecutions
was 768, of which number 45 were for the cruel and revolting
practice of dog and cock ﬁghting; while in 1,426 other cases
the ofﬁcers of the Society prevented the use of animals that
were unﬁt for work from various causes.

AMBULANCES.

The ambulances, of which there are three, have been the
means of removing from the streets and conveying to places of
shelter no less than 311 disabled horses during the past twelve
months; while, in order to terminate the sufferings of others

that were crippled or disabled past recovery, the painful neces- _

sity has arisen for the merciful destruction of 1,816 more.

 

 

 




 

 

 

13

With feelings of the deepest sorrow we convey the sad in-
telligence that the records of the Society for the year 1883, re-
call painful memories associated with the loss of many valued
members, prominent among whom may be mentioned the re-
moval from the sphere of his usefulness of one of our honored
Vice-Presidents,.the benevolent and lamented Peter Cooper.

A warm friend of the cause, Mr. Royal Phelps, has been
chosen to ﬁll the vacancy occasioned by his death ; while
Messrs. Joseph H. Choate and William Laimbeer have been'
elected members of the Executive Committee.

Since the last annual report was issued, 140. names have
been added to the roll of members of the Society, and we may
cite as examples of the growing interest in the welfare of the
animal world, the organization of branch societies in Broome,
Cayuga, and Niagara Counties, besides the appointment in
other sections of the State of twenty-eight additional special
agents.

STOCK YARDS.

On the arrival of cars loaded with live stock at the several
depots during the early hours of the morning, ofﬁcers of the
Society are present to prevent abuse, and see that the animals.
are properly cared for pending their removal to the abattoirs.

“ BAGGED ” COWS.

The pernicious practice of bringing cows to market for
sale with their udders distended with milk, for the purpose of
enhancing their value, has been almost entirely suppressed.
Ofﬁcers of the Society are always on hand awaiting the arriVal
of boats engaged in their transportation, and before the animals
are landed their progeny are allowed the natural food of which
they have been deprived. Cows without young are relieved
of their sufferings arising from this cause by being milked.

SLAUGHTER HOUSES.
A constant supervision is kept over the many slaughter
houses of the city, with a View of preventng abuse and mitigat-
ing the sufferings of animals slaughtered for human food.

 




l4
SHIPMENT OF LIVE STOCK.

The exportation of cattle and sheep to foreign countries
has assumed large proportions during the past few years. Ves-
sels of all descriptions, regardless of accommodationshare in
great demand to transport the stock, and but for the presence
of the Society’s ofﬁcers at the time of shipment, much, cruelty
would be inﬂicted by'the cattle drovers. The absence of clubs
and steel-pointed sticks, formerly used to prod and beat the
animals, is evidence of what is being done in that direction.
The ofﬁcers measure each stall provided for the animals on
shipboard, and permit only as many to be placed therein as the
space will comfortably allow. The quality and quantity of
food and water are thoroughly examined, also the attendance
provided in case of sickness or accident on the voyage. Dur-
ing the summer months sheep and cattle were carried on the
upper decks of vessels, but when the cold weather set in the
practice was discontinued, pursuant to a vigorous communica-
tion from President Bergh, addressed to commanders of cattle
carrying steamers, wherein he cited the statute governing such

cases.

MISCELLANEOUS WORK.

Night and day a careful watch is kept over the various
street railroad and stage lines to prevent as much as possible the
over-driving and other abuse of the horses. The several mar-
kets are ofﬁcially visited from time to time, and much cruelty
to poultry and game is prevented.

CANAL WORK.

Experienced ofﬁcers were detailed from the Society’s head-
quarters to operate along the Erie and other canals of the
State. They found many horses and mules engaged in towing
heavily laden canal boats while suffering from fearful wounds
upon their breasts, occasioned by the chaﬁng of their collars.
The drivers and owners were arrested and punished, and the
animals temporarily suspended from work.

THE BROOKLYN AGENCY.

Through the generosity of many philanthropic residents
of Brooklyn, aided by the exertions of Superintendent J. R.
Pye, the Brooklyn Agency has become a self-sustaining and
efﬁcient organization. During the past year its ofﬁcers have
effected 177 arrests, and in other instances have caused the re—
moval of 375 disabled animals from harness. Fifty-three
horses were transported from the streets in the Agency’s Am-
bulance, and its ofﬁcers humanely destroyed 383 that were in—‘

'jured or diseasedpast recovery.

INTEREST IN THE WORK.

The frequent requests for information in regard to the
organization of Societies for the Protection of Animals, which
have been recently received from all parts of America, as well
as from the most remote countries of the Eastern Hemisphere,
convey the gratifying assurance that the rights of those dumb.
creatures which serve man so faithfully and receive so little
in return, are being more generally recognized and respected
throughout the whole civilized world,

CHECK REINS.

This fashion, causing so much suffering to the horse, is
gradually being discarded, as may be noticed in the streets of
our city, particularly on the part of the wealthy owners of
horses.

CLIPPING.

This senseless custom, under the advice of the lazy groom,
is still practiced on the part of numerous persons, notwith-
standing the danger and cruelty made apparent by our illus-
trated publications on the subject.

TRAP SHOOTING.

It is gratifying to report that the unsportsmanlike and
barbarous pastime of pigeon shooting languishes.

THE U. S. POSTAL SERVICE IN NEW YORK.

If the citizens and strangers in this metropolis are daily
shocked at the exhibition of the rickety, tasteless and ridiculous
vehicles drawn by lame, sick andrsore horses over the streets of
the city, bearing the mails of the Federal Government, they
would also be amazed to learn that this imperfect service has
been tolerated for years by the Government, on the ground
that so long as the contractor succeeds in delivering to its place
of destination the postal matter conﬁded to him, all obliga-
tions of a humane and becoming character are unimportant.
But these dilapidated circus vans have now become so odious,
and the delays so frequent, that the protests of this Society
and the public are in a fair way of being attended to.

FINALLY.

Notwithstanding these and other adverse circumstances,
I have to report a steady progress in all the affairs of the So-
ciety, which has now become an acknowledged power in the

land for good.
, HENRY BERGH,
