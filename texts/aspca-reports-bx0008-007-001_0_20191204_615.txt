LADIES AND GENTLEMEN:

The calendar of time records the ﬂight of twenty-one years
since the organization of this Society.

It is not necessary to encroach long upon the notice of its
members with detailed statements, which may be had on refer-
ence to the subjoined report of the Superintendent.

The President may, however, be excused for dwelling a
moment on a subject not therein referred to. What a parody on
our boasted civilization it seems to be, that mankind should
seek for their pleasures in the sufferings and death of defenseless
animals I The degrading scenes of the Spanish bull-ﬁght, where
the sovereign and his court lend their approving presence to the
demoralization of the people, down to the cheap and popular
“ sport” of rat-baiting—as these assaults on reason are char-
acterized——the animating spirit is ever the same.

As the sustaining attraction of the race course is gambling,

 




 

 

 

 

 

  

6

so these cruel orgies rely on blood-shedding for their existence;
were there no death, no mutilation, no suffering, they could not
survive a day.

Among all such pastimes of the idle and the illiterate, that of
fox-hunting takes highest rank in the unique sublimity of its
ridiculous stupidity.

A terriﬁed little fox pursued by a mob of men, women, horses,
and dogs is a spectacle that has no parallel in the moving pano-
rama of human follies. .

And now the American people are threatened with another
“foreign sport,” known as “pig-sticking.” In ancient times,
by order of royalty this enlightened fun was called “ wild boar
hunting.” The old Romans used to pursue the animal on foot,
but it is thought that our huntsmen of the fox and “the anise-
seed bag ” have too high an appreciation of danger to attempt
any such exposure.

In later times, William the Conquerer punished with the loss
of their eyes all persons who dared to infringe on the cherished
sport of that half-barbarous sovereign—he wanted to do all the
sticking himself.

‘But to the point. A foreign citizen, having, as it may be
assumed, the pleasure, culture, and national progress of our
people at heart, has unselﬁshly undertaken to provide our native
sporting population with this “classic pastime.” It is hardly
necessary to observe that this generous stranger has been notiﬁed
that the great beneﬁt which he meditates bestowing on a portion
of our fellow-citizens is forbidden by the laws of the State, and
that this Society, not sharing his wild enthusiasm, will on the
ﬁrst overt act on his part repeat the legal ceremonies with
which it received the serious attempts, a few years ago, of some

Spaniards to inoculate the education of our innocent people with
the inestimable blessings of bull-ﬁghting I

HENRY BERGH,
President.

“OUR ANIMAL FRIENDS.”

This Journal, published under the auspices of the Society, is
now in the ﬁfteenth year of its existence. During its publica-
tion it has steadily kept in view the cause it advocates, and in
its columns the more important proceedings of the Society have
been given to the public. Short stories, sketches, and other in-
teresting miscellany, appropriate to the character of the paper,
have tended largely to increase its circulation, and make it
valuable as a household journal.

Annual subscription for the paper, ONE DOLLAR.

Geo. Will Johnston, Editor.

SPRING HOUSE,
RIOHEIELD SPRINGS, June 9th, 1886.
DEAR SIR:—-As a guest at Richﬁeld Springs last season, you
will doubtless remember a large stone drinking fountain on the
western shore of Canadarago Lake, the inscription upon which is,
“ A merciful man will be merciful to his beast.”
A similar fountain has been made for the eastern side of the

lake, and with your permission the following inscription will be
out upon its face:
IN HONOR OE

HENRY BERGH,
THE FRIEND OF ANIMALS.
With great respect,
Your obedient servent,
T. R. PROOTOR.

NEW YORK, June 11th, 1886.

DEAR SIR:—I am in receipt of a most ﬂattering note from you,
tendering me an honor, whose chief attraction is that I should
be esteemed worthy of it.

If twenty years of devotion to a cause which is no more my
privilege to serve, than it is that of the humblest citizen in the
land, prompt this high distinction, let it be done as an incentive
to others to follow my example.

Gratefully, and with great respect,
HENRY BERGH.

SUPERINTENDENT’S REPORT.

 

The following report of the work done by the Society’s ofﬁcers
during the past year is necessarily partial and incomplete.

It would be difﬁcult to give in detail the almost infinite Oper-
ations of those charged with the prevention of abuse of animals.
In the language of the statute, the word “animal,” while it
does not include the human race, includes every other living
creature, hence it may be readily inferred that those duties are
diversiﬁed and numerous.

STOCK YARDS AND SLAUGHTER HOUSES.

Ofﬁcers have been detailed to inspect the condition of. all ani-
mals arriving at the several stock yards and other places, and to
insist on their proper treatment.

Over 10,000 milch cows, calves and sheep, brought to this
city during the summer months on river boats, were appropri-
ately cared for.

The several abattoirs have been weekly visited, and a swift and
merciful death demanded for each animal; and many reasonable
improvements with regard to the treatment of animals awaiting
slaughter have been readily adopted, at our suggestion, by the
proprietors, and it may be conﬁdently asserted that the spirit
of mercy has penetrated even the abattoir.

TRANSPORTATION OF LIVE STOCK.

This industry is continually on the increase, and our ofﬁcers.
have been vigilant in the duties aﬁected thereby.

POULTRY.

A persistent warfare has been necessary against shippers and
consignees of live poultry, compelling them to practise humanity
to the feathered race, as well as justice to the consumers of their
ﬂesh.

BLEEDING CALVES.

This needless and atrocious crime is, owing to the vigilance
of our ofﬁcers, almost entirely suppressed.

DOG AND COOK FIGHTING.

Dog and cock ﬁghters ﬁnd it unsafe to indulge their brutal in-
stincts within the jurisdiction of this county, by reason of the
constant surveillance kept over the places Where they congregate.

CANAL WORK.

The'canals throughout the State were thoroughly policed by
the Society’s ofﬁcers, and many boatmen were arrested and ﬁned
for compelling horses and mules, while sore and unﬁt for work,
to draw heavily laden boats.

MISCELLANEOUS DUTY.

Places of amusement where animals are employed have been
ofﬁcially visited, likewise the railroad, stage, and other large
stables. Horses employed by night hack—men, and those
attached to fashionable equipages, have been inspected, es—
pecially during the winter months, and when found un-
blanketed or otherwise neglected, the drivers or owners were
«compelled to stable the animals or provide necessary covering.
Highly checked horses have been relieved, and their drivers
warned against the use of cruel bits and bearing reins.

The “Horse Market,” where old decrepit horses and mules
are brought to be sold, likewise the city “dog pound,” have
received ofﬁcial attention.

 

 




 

 

11

It having been publicly announced that a horse belonging to
.a circus company, would be compelled to walk across Niagara
Falls on a wire rope stretched over the chasm, ofﬁcers were
sent to that place with instructions to stop the proposed ex-
hibition. They carried out their orders to the letter, much to
the surprise and discomﬁture of the showmen.

Communications were addressed to the several ferry com-
panies, requesting that suitable protection from the wind be
provided on their boats, for horses exposed during the winter
months while crossing the rivers.

The humane request in several instances has been complied
with, and others will deubtless soon follow the example.

The veterinary medicine chest, containing bandages, lotions,
surgical and other instruments, now carried in the Society’s
patrol wagon, has been of inestimable service.

GLANDERS AND FAROY.

The spread of these contagious and fatal maladies has been
in a measure checked.

AMBULANCE WORK.

The ambulances were required on 412 occasions to remove
from the streets, horses and other animals which had been in-
jured, or otherwise disabled. These vehicles, along with
the derrick—the latter being used to raise animals out of
excavations and other dangerous places—are known as the
Society’s animal life-saving equipment.
