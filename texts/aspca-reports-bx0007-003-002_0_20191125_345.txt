REPORT OF THE EXECUTIVE:
COMMITTEE.

 

The Executive Committee, in presenting their report for the-
year 1887, feel assured that the record of this and kindred as-
sociations in AmErica will demonstrate that at no previous
period in its history has the cause of animal protection made
more satisfactory progress than during the twelve months which
complete the twenty-second year of the existence of this Society-

The growing public interest in the welfare of those useful
creatures, which are so necessary to our comfort and pleasure,
ﬁnds expression in the kinder treatment which they now receive,
and in the frequent recurrence of exhibitions of animals, which
have met with unqualiﬁed public favor.

As the statistics published elsewhere in the report of the
superintendent serve to demonstrate the necessity for that watch-
ful supervision which the Society exercises over those dumb-
beings, whose right to exemption from needless suffering it
is our constituted mission .to enforce, this Committee does not
deem it expedient to refer to such details of the work as are-
therein contained.

That so many instances of cruelty should still exist, as that re-
port reveals, is not attributable to an increase in the cruel treat»
ment of animals, but is due to the greater facilities which the
Society now possesses for detecting such offences.

Forty-seven names have been added to the roll of membership
during the past year. We are also greatly pained to record the-
decease of those members whose names appear in the obituary
notice which accompanies this report.

Our sister association in the State of California has also sus-
tained a great loss in the death of its president, the late Joseph W.
Winans, who was one of the pioneers of the humane cause on
the Paciﬁc slope.

 

 

 




 

9

There are now forty-four Societies for the Prevention of
Cruelty to Animals in North and South America, of which
number thirty-three are in the United States. These associa.
tions, with their numerous branches, present a powerful bulwark
against cruelty in every form.

At present the parent society has ﬁfteen branches and two
hundred and thirty-one special agents distributed throughout
the State.

The Agency at 415 Fulton Street, Brooklyn, has taken an
active part in reforming abuses in that city, as the following
statement, taken from the report of Superintendent Clark for
the year 1887, indicates:

Cases prosecuted in the Courts ........................... 110
Disabled Animals temporarily suspended from work ...... 219
Horses, disabled past recovery, humanely destroyed ..... 734
Small Animals, disabled past recovery, humanely de-
stroyed ........ . ..................................... 145
Disabled H0rses removed from the Streets in the Ambu—
lances ....................... . ........................ 140
Complaints received and investigated .................... 833

In the performanee of this work we still meet with numerous
phases of cruelty, which involve great and widespread suffering.
Among these we may instance the wanton and indiscriminate
slaughter of the buffalo; the transportation to our western plains
of vast herds of domestic cattle, where they are left without
provender or shelter, except such as they can ﬁnd during the-
winter season, when thousands starve and freeze to death; the
Wholesale slaughter of those beautiful and useful birds which
add so many blessings to our own existence and lend such a ma-
terial assistance to the farmer in protecting his crops from the
ravages of insects; and also, the overcrowding and improper
feeding of cattle in transit from the west.

Let us hope that with the moral and material development of
our country the causes which are productive of these and other
cruelties will forever disappear.

By order of the Executive Committee,
HENRY BERGH, JR.,
Secretary.

 




 

 

 

 

 

 

 

"SUPERINTENDENT’S REPORT.

In submitting this, the twenty-second annual report of the
general Work accomplished by the Society’s ofﬁcers during the
past year, in the enforcement of the humane statutes of the
State, and the protection and prevention of cruelty to helpless
dumb creatures, I would premise by saying that the public
abuse of animals is of more rare occurrence. Owing to the vast
improvements going on in the upper districts of this county,
requiring the employment of a large number of horses and
mules, the presence of our ofﬁcers cannot be dispensed with,
although matters nearer home seemingly suffer temporary
neglect. No animal is too insigniﬁcant for the protection of
the Society. The following will, in a condensed form, show
What has been done during the past year.

STOCK YARDS AND SLAUGHTER HOUSES.

Ofﬁcers have been present on the arrival of trains and boats
carrying live stock, and have insisted on the proper treatment
of the animals where necessary. \

The conveying of sheep and calves from the stock-yards to
the slaughter houses and the loading and unloading of them has
been reformed by our ofﬁcers. . ~

The abattoirs of the city have been regularly inspected, and
all merciful measures demanded, to insure a swift death for

creatures awaiting slaughter.

The practice of bringing milch cows, with their udders dis-
tended, to market for sale, by unscrupulous dealers, is gradually

becoming a thing of the past. Over half a million head of live
stock was inspected by our ofﬁcers during the past year.

Under the above title every other detail of our work not here-
tofore mentioned is embodied. Markets, night and day hack-

Where animals are employed, the horse market where worthless
horses and mules are brought to be sold, likewise the city “dog-
pound” and “dog-catchers” have received ofﬁcial attention.
All complaints received pertaining to the work of animal protec-
tion have) been ﬁnvestigated, and when reported é“ well-
founded,” prompt ofﬁcial action has been rendered.

SHIPMENT OF LIVE STOCK.

All vessels carrying cattle and other animals to foreign coun-
. tries have been visited by our representatives and the regulations
prescribed by President Bergh several years ago, with regard to
space, food, and water, have been rigidly insisted on.

DOG, COOK-FIGHTS, AND RAT BAITS.

These demoralizing exhibitions have been almost entirely
suppressed in this city. It is most difﬁcult to discover con-
templated contests between animals even in this, and the more
isolated counties, by reason of the secrecy with which they are

conducted.
CANAL WORK.

Reports received from our local agents, residing along the
routes of the several canals of the State, indicate that the con-
dition of the animals employed in towing canal-boats has im-
proved under their constant supervision.

GLANDERS AND FARCY.

These incurable diseases, contagious to man as well as beast,
are the consequences of neglect, bad food, and damp, ill-ven-
tilated stables.

During the past year, several persons have been prosecuted and
convicted for selling and exposing horses. so diseased. In all
cases, animals found suffering with either of these disorders are
immediately destroyed.

AMBULANCES AND DERRICK.

Five hundred and twenty-two sick and disabled animals were
removed from the streets to veterinary hospitals in our ambu-
lances, and the derrick was called into requisition to rescue
several horses from drowning and excavations in which they had
accidentally fallen. Any hour of the day or night they are
available, and can be had on application at the headquarters
of the Society, with an ofﬁcer in charge.

LADIES AND GENTLEMEN:

I will not intrude my personal ailings on you, further than to
say that I have not enjoyed as ample a share of health and
strength, during the past year, as I could have desired.

I have, however, discharged all my duties to the best of my
powers, and I ask your indulgence for my defects.

Permit me to renew the assurance of my profound respect,
and to wish you, in all your relations to life and its affairs, a
hearty and prosperous “ Happy New Year.”

_The condition of our Society in all respects admits of mutual
congratulations, as the respective reports of committees prove.

It may be conﬁdently asserted that during the long term of
its existence of twenty-two years, not a deliberate instance
of disrespect, or infringement of law has ever occurred, and
that while it steadily pursues the merciful labor of protecting
the helpless from abuse, it is an Agent of Moral Education to
our own race as well.

HENRY BERGH,
