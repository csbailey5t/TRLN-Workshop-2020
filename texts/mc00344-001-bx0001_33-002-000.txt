Dear Christine:
I appreciate very much your beautiful Christmas

greeting and hope that you and your family had a most en-
joyable holiday season.

I am concerned as you are with regard to the lab;

animal legislation.

I certainly agree with your statement that any
bill must meet the minimum basic principles as outlined, and,
of course, we would all like to see additional requirements.
However, bills such as the Randall Bill would probably be
impossible to have passed at this time.

At the time the A.H,A. Bill (copy of which I do
not have) was presented to the Board of Directors, I spoke
against it as I felt that it was inadequate, and asked that
my objections be entered in the record. It is no breach
of my position as a director to say this, as I stated at
the time that I would have to tell outsiders why I had
objected to the particular bill.

It seems to me that all the bills that I have re-
viewed place too much power in one official. It would be
so easy to have the wrong person who could control, direct
and prevent the proper enforcement of the law.

Dear Mrs. Stevens:

You have a perfect right to think I am a "renegade", in
View of my long silence following receipt of your letter

of June 29th. I'm sure you will forgive my seeming neglect,
however, when you hear of some of my woes. We have had
managerial troubles at our Shelter, my sister Jo, is.half
sick, I had an attack of bursitis for the first and I hope
the last time. It really put me out of commission for quite
a while. The head of our firm was appointed as a judge of
the Fourth Circuit Court of Appeals whbh has meant more
work and responsibility for the remaining members of our
firm. These are a few of the many things that have caused
me to fall far behind with my correspondence.

In answer to your letter regarding the compulsory humane
slaughter bill:

I wrote again to our congressman, Robert T. Ashmore to the
effect that we knew we could count on him to do everything

he could to see that a compulsory bill was enacted. He had
written me on a previous occasion saying that he would be glad
to support the bill. I know him personally and know that we
can count on him. I did not receive a letter in reply to my
last communication beacuse it really did not call for a reply.

I wrote to the Hon John w. McCormack and enclose his non-
commiﬂal reply. '

Some of our members have written and I shall endeavor to secure
copies of the.replies received.

I am writing to Mrs. w.C. Wannamaker, Jr., whose address is
8H0 CQﬂaadM1(;¢(5 Orangeburg, 8.0. and am confident she will do
all she can. If you have time, I suggest you also write her.

I have never been able to do much with Charleston, although I
understand that many years ago they had a very good Society there. ,
I suggest you write to Mrs. Rhett, 48 Elizabeth St., and Mrs. V"
Dorothy C. Monserrat, 3O Moultrie St. I do not know them ,
personally but understand that they have beengquite interested.

 




It will explain some of our difficulties in getting as much
publicity in Greenville regarding this matter as we should when
I tell you this: There is a large packing company here by the
name of Balentine Packing Company which is very powerful and
closely connected with the city authorities by reason of the
marriage of some of the Balentines to some of the top city
officials. There is also a connection with the paper and

both papers, morning and evening, are owned by the same outfit!
Aren't we tied in a knot!

I

Congratulations on the fine work you are doing and on the
bulletins you issue.

The road followed by humane workers is a rough one as you
and I know. It is full of thorns and many obstacles. In
spite of discouragements, misunderstandings and-hardships,
I expect, nevertheless, to do all I can to promote the
humane cause as long as I live.

With every good wish, I am,

very sincerely yours,

W

Thank you very much forﬁvery informative but distressing
letter of September 28th.

I regret that I do not have the totals of the votes on the
second ballot at the Atlanta Convation. As soon as I can
can get in touch with.Mr. Steve Kelly I'll find out if he
by any chance has this information. You may recall him

as one of those who helped count votes etc.

The situation is really serious because of the propaganda
put forth by the Old Guard. None of the Greenville members
will be influenced by anything the Old Guard has to say,
but there are members in North Carolina who have been
frightened by the reference to Mr. Fred Myers'connection
with the CIO, and have also, in my opinion, been greatly
influenced by Mr. Treleaven. ‘

There are two members in N. C. I would suggest you write:
Mrs. Boyd Quarles, 516 Dock St.,'Wilmington,N.C. and Mrs.
Fred Hester, 7 Griffing Road, Asheville,N.C. They are
both very loyal to the reform group and I know them per-
sonally. ‘Mrs. Hester, I believe, will attend the Convention.
Mrs. Quarles may not because of the illness of her sister
and for the further reason that her application for member-
ship was denied. She was a delegate from her Society last
year, but not an individual member. This year, as I under-
stand it, her Society could not become a member because it
could not fill out the questionnaire. I'm sure, however,
that she will do anything she can tohelp the cause.

Thank you for the list of S.C. members. I have sent each one
of them a letter today, except the Greenville members, all of
whom already know the story andlseveral are planning to come to
Grand Rapids. My sister, Mrs. Dennhardt, will probably not
attend because she and I cannot leave home at the same time.
Mr. Sublette has had a heart attach,and another member has had
a nervous breakdnwn, and while she has recovered physically,

is hard up financially.

Very sincerely,

July 28, 1955

Miss J. M. Perry
BOX 2066
GreenVille, South Carolina

Dear Miss Perry,

I have just returned from a trip to find your letters of July 13th and diet
both of which I was very glad to have, although I was sorry to learn of yeur sister's
illness and of the distress which it is undoubtedly causing you. I hope she is mahiig
progress back to good health new.

-I appreciated your sending me Miss Cepp's-statement. It is extremely sad that
she should be circulating this kind of inflamatory report, but it is not surprising.
You have undoubtedly seen the costly booKlet brought out on this same subject by Harvey
Metcalf with a picture of Miss Copp as the Froﬁispiece. About two years ago Miss Copp
came to see me in New York. She was then just about to become president of the Vancouve r
SPCA, and we discussed ways and means of improving laboratory animal welfare very thordéhly.
The "Vancouver Agreement" with the medical school was then in effect. I provided her
with relevant literature; we corresponded occasionally; and about a year later she and
Mr. Ricardo and I visited the University of British Columbia Medical School together,
consulted with the Dean and several professors, and during all this time, so far as I
knew, there was no question concerning the value of the Vancouver agreement which provides
that dogs which are due to be destroyed at the City Pound (not SPCA operated) may be used
at the Medical School provided that they are first put under full anesthesia from which
they are never permitted to recover but pass directly into death. I want to emphasize
that this agreement prevents the use of pound dogs for any experiment which could
cause pain or even the slightest distress since the animals must be anesthetized and
killed while under the anesthetic. If this agreement should be annulled, as Miss Copp now
wants it to be, nothing would stand in the way of the medical school‘s getting dogs
from the pound for any type of use. Furthermore, the agreement provides for inspection
by an SPCA representative of all animals: dogs bred at the University and used for chronic
experiments, cats, monkeys,raEEits, guinea pigs, mice and rats. This inspection, naturally,
does not provide complete protection for all of these animals. It does, however, give
an excellent opportunity for missionary work, and Mr. Ricardo, who had done most of
it, is especially to be commended for the dogged way he has worked and , in most instan-
ces, obtained the improvements he asked for in matters concerning haniing, housing and
care of animals of different kinds. Cf enormous importance for the future of laboratory
animal welfare have been his annual lectures to medical students on the humane treatment
of animals. He was successful in getting these lectures made a regular part of the
curriculum; that is, attendance by the students is compulsory, and there is nothing
wishyawashy about the lectures. You might be interested inreading an outline of one of
them, and if so, I should be glad to send it to you.

Exactly what led Miss Copp to become so exceedingly hustile to the whole
set—up, I won't try to say, for I don't entirely know. In any case, she resigned from
the SPCA and sent out mimeographed statements very widely condemning the Vancouver
agreement. As I recall them, the only point which she mentinned which seemed to me to
constitute a valid criticism was a single case in which an animal was sent to the labo—
ratory before the regular holding period in the pound was up. This was a matter of pound
administration which certainly called for correction, but so far as mistreatment of
pound animals at the laboratory was concerned, there was no indication that such a thing
had ever happened.

Dear Mrs. Stevens:

Thank you for yours of December 29 telling
me the good news that the Red Cross Manual -
"Nutrition Course in Secondary Schools" has been
discontinued. The humane cause does get a break
occasionally.

There is to be a Board meeting of the A. H. A.
January 21 and we should have something to report

following that date.
With best wishes, I am,

Very sincerely yours,

J. M. Perry

Animal Welfare Institute
