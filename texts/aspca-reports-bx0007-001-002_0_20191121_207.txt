HEADQUARTERS OF THE SOCIETY,
COR. FOURTH AVENUE AND 22D STREET,
NEW YORK, JAN. RT, 1886.

LADIES AND GENTLEMEN:

The twentieth illewr of the oﬁ‘ieial e90—

istence of this Society terminates to-
day!

It forms no part Of my purpose, on this occasion, to dwell on
what has been done by it during that long period Of time, nor
yetto speak of what remains to be done.

The memories associated with its rise and progress, its vicissi-
tudes, its benefactions, and its triumphs have now become a part
of the written history of our country, and its services to civiliza-
tion are more potently and eloquently discussed, through the
merciful and enlightened progress of. the events with which it has
been identiﬁed, than anything the undersigned can utter.

Various methods Of more or less showy and attractive char-
acter, have been employed by worthy associations to signalize

 




 

6

such occasions, but it will be freely conceded that neither
pageantry nor festival can so fully demonstrate the beauty of
good deeds as the practical and visible evidences thereof. While
the monuments which a grateful people erect in commemora—
tion of a cause inevitably perish, the great principles underlying
them remain and are indestructible.

Thus let it be with ours. Born—so to speak—amidst revolu-
tionary and fraternal strife, our people soon appreciated their
obligations to those humble creatures, created for their service,
and committed to their merciful consideration, by respecting
the laws enacted in their behalf. What once seemed to be an
abstract sentimental idea, has crystallized into the practice of
that most engaging of the human virtues—mercy. ’

If pride and exultation are ever excusable, they are made so by
the admirable record of your Society, during the two decades now
brought to a close. Through its example and assistance, more
than three-fourths of the States and Territories of our own
country, have adopted the laws and practices which govern it,
and foreign nations have applied for, and received the beneﬁts of
our great experience, and have instituted similar organizations
within their own borders, and have honored this, the parent
Society of America, by adopting the seal and emblem it employs.

Finally, it is the earnest prayer of all right-thinking men and
women, that when another score of years shall mark its expiration
on the dial of time, cause as great, or even greater than ours,
may exist, for congratulation on the part of those, to whom this
civilizing and humane work shall have been conﬁded.

HENRY BERGH,
President.

TREASURER’S REPORT.

NEW YORK, January lst, 1886.
To “ Tire American Society for the Prevention of Ornelty to Animals : ”

Together with this report, I beg leave to submit the usual ﬁnancial‘statement, showing more in
detail the sources from which the revenues of your Society have been derived, and also the various
accounts under which its funds have been disbursed during the past year.

From December 31st, 1884, to December 31st, 1885, its income, exclusive of the balances
brought forward from the previous year, was $34,582.87.

The total amount of disbursements during that time, including the amount of $4,402.88 ex-
pended in improvements on its real estate, was $25,632.92, leaving a balance in the Union Trust Co.
of $18,797.06, and Petty Cash Drawer of $22.72, a total of $18,819.78.

Your Society has invested, exclusive of its real estate, the following amounts:

$11,000 on Bond and Mortgage on the premises 161 West 27th Street, New York.

$4,000 in 40 shares of New York Central and Hudson River R. R. 00. Stock.

$2,500 in First Mortgage Bonds of the Burlington, Cedar Rapids and N. R. R. Co.

$2,000 in Bonds of the City of Poughkeepsie Asylum.

Owing to a strict observance of the conservative policy which has always characterized the
management of the ﬁnances of this Institution, and inﬂuenced by which the expenditures have been
limited to the amount of the net income, it is now, as it has been in the past, free from debt.

Respectfully submitted. CHARLES LANIER, Treasurer.

SUPERINTENDENT’S REPORT.

 

In submitting the following statement of the work performed
during the past year, I would premise by saying, that to enumer—
ate all that has been accomplished would require more time and
space than can be accorded to this report.

While many cases of outrageous cruelty have been prosecuted,
it is not the purpose of this report to ishock the sensibilities of
its readers with their painful details, but to give, in as condensed
form as possible, the character and magnitude of the work.

STOCK YARDS AND SLAUGHTER HOUSES.

The condition and treatment of live stock, on arrival at the
several depots in this city, have been carefully inspected by the
Society’s ofﬁcers specially detailed for that purpose; whose pres-
ence, while preventing possible abuse of the animals, also
tended to serve the public in a sanitary point of view.

A close watch has been observed on all river boats bringing
milch cows, calves, and sheep to market during the summer
months, and much cruelty has been prevented by our ofﬁcers,
who were present at the landing-places on the arrival of the
vessels.

Over 8,000 cows, calves, and sheep have been inspected and
properly cared for.

The slaughter. houses have also been ofﬁcially visited each
week, and many suggestions from President Bergh, with regard
to the comfort and treatment of animals awaiting slaughter,
have been adopted by the proprietors.

Owing to the vigilance of our ofﬁcers, the bleeding of calves
for the purpose of bleaching the ﬂesh, has been greatly diminished.

 

 

 




 

 

   

12 ,
TRANSPORTATION OF LIVE STOCK.

An unusually large number of cattle and other animals, was
shipped from this port to foreign countries during the past year,
which necessitated extra vigilance on the part of our ofﬁcers, in
order that the animals should be provided with proper accommo-
dations, food, and water while on the voyage.

Over 60,000 head of live stock were exported during the year.

POULTRY.

Considerable trouble still exists with regard to the manner live-
poultry is shipped to this city, owing to shippers residing outside
of this State being ignorant of the law governing the carrying
of animals. .

The evil is gradually being remedied by our rigid mode of
procedure.

DOG AND COOK FIGHTING.

The so-called sports Of dog and cock ﬁghting are still fur-
tively carried on in remote localities, outside this city where it.
is almost impossible for the Ofﬁcers to approach without dis-7
covery. Several of these unlawful gatherings, however, have-
been broken up, the paraphernalia seized and conﬁscated, and
the principals arrested and punished.

CANAL WORK.

This special line of duty was entrusted, during the last season,
to the agents of the Society residing along the line of the several
canals. Many owners and captains of boats have been prosecuted
for permitting their horses and mules to be worked while ina
disabled condition.

MISCELLANEOUS DUTY.

Under the above head may be mentioned the inspection of
horses employed in drawing streetrailway cars, which, by reason
of the operation of several new lines, has greatly augmented our
labors.

Hackmen and others who ply their calling between midnight
:and morning, with broken-down horses have experienced the
wholesome vigilance of our oﬂ‘icers.

Farmers, truckmen, expressmen, peddlers, drivers of fashion-
.:able equipages, and others having the custody of animals, were
.made to respect the humane laws of the State.

The “horse market,” where worn-out horses are brought to
‘be sold, has been under surveillance, and many diseased and
broken—down creatures have been humanely disposed of.

The “city dog pound” received constant attention, and the
animals captured and taken there, pursuant to municipal ordi-
nance, were properly cared for, while awaiting their ﬁnal
(destruction.

GLANDERS AND FAROY.

These terrible and incurable diseases have been unusually pre-
valent for some time past, and the able report of one of our
veterinary surgeons conﬁrms our experience with these maladies.
’The precise cause of these dangerous diseases is not satisfactorily
.accounted for, but it is safe to assert that neglect and abuse,
,as in nearly all physical disorders, are the initiatory causes. They
are contagious in their nature, and dangerous to human life as
well as to animals. The deplorable practice 011 the part of owners
of horses in not providing blankets to throw over their animals
While standing in the streets, often in a dripping perspiration,
ﬁnds, perhaps, a reasonable explanation of the existence of the
diseases.

AMBULANCE WORK.

The numerous animals which have been rescued from pain-
ful deaths through the instrumentality of these vehicles, and in
many cases restored to usefulness, may be inferred when it is
stated that during the year 287 have been picked up and re-
moved to shelter and medical treatment.

SE CRETARY’S REPORT.

 

During the twenty years which have succeeded-the initiatory
steps to introduce into the Western Hemisphere the civilizing
work of Animal Protection, the friends of the humane cause have-
witnessed with satisfaction its steady development and success.

The South American States are beginning to vie with their sis-
ters of the North, in disseminating those principles of humanity
which teach the practice of mercy to all living creatures.

There has been in existence in the Argentine Republic, for
several years past, an active organization similar to your own ;
which, under the able guidance of its distinguished president,
General D. F. Sarmiento, has lately given additional proof of its
efﬁciency, by the purchase in this city of a handsome ambulance
for the transportation of disabled animals.

A similar society has been created in the empire of Brazil,
under the patronage of his majesty the Emperor, and numerous
citizens of rank and social distinction. Through the personal
co-operation of Mr. Valente, his majesty’s representative at
Washington, Mr. Bergh was enabled to initiate proceedings,
which have culminated very auspiciously in that empire, and
have been recognized by the Emperor in a letter of thanks to
our president.

In other distant countries active measures are in progress,
which, it is hoped, will, ere long, extend to the animal creation
Within their borders, protection from the cruelties to which they
are at present subjected.

The State of Florida, which, like a few other commonwe ealths
of this republic, had remained a passive spectatm of the growth
of the humane work in other parts of our Union, has within the
past year recognized the rights— of animals to protection from
abuse, there being now in operation in that State two societies.
for the accomplishment of that beneﬁcent object.
